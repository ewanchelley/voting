Given the choice of two rankings, a voter is said to 'prefer' the ranking that has the smallest Kendall distance from their own ranking. If the Kendall distance is the same to the two rankings, the voter does not have a preference and is said to abstain.

A ranking is more popular than another in the weak sense if it is preferred by a majority of the voters. A ranking can be said to be 'weakly popular' if no other ranking is more popular in the weak sense.

A ranking is more popular in the strong sense if it is preferred by a majority of the non-abstaining voters. A ranking can be said to be 'strongly popular' if no other ranking is more popular in the strongly sense.

If a ranking is strongly popular then it must also be weakly popular, however it is possible for a ranking to be weakly strongly but not strongly popular. If a ranking is weakly popular then it must also be a Kemeny consensus. Given the candidates: A, B, C, D, E and F and the 6 rankings:

R1: C, A, B, E, F, D
R2: A, B, C, F, D, E
R3: B, C, A, D, E, F
R4: A, B, C, D, E, F
R5: B, A, C, F, D, E
R6: B, C, A, D, F, E

Suppose we want to find out if the ranking σ1: A, B, C, D, E, F is weakly popular and if it is strongly popular?

The ranking needs to be compared against every other possible ranking of the candidates to see if there exists a ranking that is more popular (in either the weak sense or strong sense).

An alternative ranking is σ2: B, C, A, F, D, E. Using the Kendall distance, we can check which of these rankings (σ1 or σ2) is preferred by each voter:

Voters Rankings Distance to σ1 Distance to σ2 Preferred ranking
R1: C, A, B, E, F, D 4 4 Abstain
R2: A, B, C, F, D, E 2 2 Abstain
R3: B, C, A, D, E, F 2 2 Abstain
R4: A, B, C, D, E, F 0 4 σ1
R5: B, A, C, F, D, E 3 1 σ2
R6: B, C, A, D, F, E 3 1 σ2

Since only 2 out of 6 voters prefer σ2 (less than half of the voters), σ2 is not more popular in the weak sense and therefore σ1 may still be weakly popular. After comparing σ1 to every other possible ranking it turns out that σ1 is in fact weakly popular, since no other ranking is stronger in the weak sense.

Because 2 out of the 3 non-abstaining voters prefer σ2 (more than half), σ2 is stronger in the strong sense and therefore σ1 is not strongly popular. A popular ranking is a notion introduced by Van Zuylen et al. (link to paper). The definition given was the same as weak popularity as explained above. Kraiczy, Cseh and Manlove extended this notion to introduce a more strict form of popularity, strong popularity (link to paper). There remain open questions about the time complexity of finding a ranking that is more popular (in the weak or strong sense) for different numbers of voters.
